The Many Deaths of Laila Starr

Author: Ram V.

Illustrator: Felipe Andrade

Publisher: BOOM! Studios

Published: 2021

Genre: Graphic Novel / Mythology / Fantasy

When humanity discovers the secret to immortality, Death herself is made redundant. Cast out of the divine and forced to live as a mortal woman named Laila Starr, she must navigate the confusion, beauty, and pain of human existence. Through a series of encounters across different lifetimes, Laila comes to understand something she never could as an immortal: it is the ending that gives everything its meaning.

Meet the Creators

Ram V.

Writer

Ram V is an award-winning author and creator of comics and graphic novels such as Grafity's Wall, These Savage Shores, and Blue in Green. Since publishing his first book in 2016, Ram's work has gone on to garner critical and popular success and multiple awards. Apart from creating original work, since 2018, Ram has also written for iconic characters and titles at DC Comics and Marvel Entertainment.

Key Works

  • Swamp Thing
  • Batman: Reptilian
  • These Savage Shores
  • Grafity's Wall

Felipe Andrade

Illustrator

Felipe Andrade is a Brazilian comic artist celebrated for his expressive linework and emotionally rich color palettes. His style blends realism with dreamlike abstraction, making him a perfect collaborator for this mythological story.

Themes & Symbolism
In this story, death is not an enemy — it is what gives life its meaning. Without an ending, nothing would matter. Laila's journey teaches her that death is not a punishment, but a gift.
Mumbai represents the chaos and beauty of human life. Millions of overlapping lives, losses, and small joys happening all at once — a constant reminder that every person carries their own universe.
Filipe Andrade uses deep blues for the divine world and warm pinks and oranges for the human world. As Laila becomes more human, the colors around her shift and soften — visually showing her transformation.
The crow is a classic symbol of death across many cultures. In this story, it becomes Laila's only companion — a darkly comic reminder of who she used to be, following her through her mortal life.
Every time Laila dies and comes back, she wakes up knowing a little more about what it means to be human. Her repeated deaths are not failures — they are lessons.
Darius is not just a character — he is a symbol of humanity's greatest fear and greatest hope. He represents the part of us that refuses to accept loss, and the question of whether cheating death would actually make us happier.
Death is not the opposite of life. It is what gives life its shape.

What Death Taught Me About Living

Before I read The Many Deaths of Laila Starr, I thought of death the way most people do — as something to avoid thinking about. But this graphic novel made me sit with it. It made me uncomfortable in the best way.

What stayed with me most is not the story of a goddess losing her job, or even the question of immortality. It is the smaller moments — Laila standing in a crowded Mumbai street, overwhelmed by the noise and color of a life she never had to experience before. Darius as a child, asking Bardhan why the soil needs turning. The crow following Laila through a city that does not know what she is.

Ram V is not asking whether death is good or bad. He is asking what we do with the time we have. And Filipe Andrade's art answers that question visually — the colors shift from cold blue to warm pink and orange as Laila becomes more human, more present, more alive. You can feel the change without being told about it.

I think what this graphic novel does best is make you feel the weight of an ordinary moment. A cup of tea. A conversation on a rooftop. A mango held in two hands. These are the things Laila learns to see. And by the end, they are the things I noticed too.

Death is not the opposite of life. It is what gives life its shape.
